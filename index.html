European Literature

A Comprehensive Review of Literary Periods

Classical Period

800 BCE - 500 CE

The Classical Period saw the birth of tragedy and comedy in Greek theater, with playwrights like Sophocles and Aristophanes, and the philosophical contributions of figures like Plato and Aristotle, which profoundly influenced Western thought. This period is characterized by its exploration of epic poetry, drama, and philosophy.

Key Works

Homer's The Iliad - An epic focusing on the final weeks of the Trojan War, highlighting the wrath of the Greek hero Achilles. Themes include honor, revenge, and fate, culminating in Achilles' battle with Hector.

Homer's The Odyssey - Follows the Greek hero Odysseus on his long journey home after the Trojan War, covering his adventures with mythical creatures, gods, and monsters as he struggles to return to Ithaca.

Virgil's Aeneid - A Latin epic poem following the journey of Aeneas, a Trojan hero after the fall of Troy, who is destined to found the city that will become Rome.

Important Facts

  • The earliest surviving European works from this period are the classical Greek epics (Iliad and Odyssey), dating from the 8th century BCE.
  • The oldest known intact book in Europe is a copy of the Saint John Gospel.
  • Christianity became widespread throughout the Roman Empire during the 3rd and 4th century, marking the beginning of religious morality in European literature.

Middle Ages (Medieval Period)

5th century - late 15th century

The fall of the Roman Empire in 467 CE marked a turning point in European literature. The Roman Catholic Church became the primary intellectual institution of the medieval period. Many surviving texts are religious in nature, including hymns, theological writings, and mystery plays depicting biblical stories.

The Middle Ages also produced secular literature in smaller quantities. Many texts, such as Y Gododdin and Beowulf, were based on myths or histories that had been recounted orally for centuries before being written down. Christian scholars often altered these stories, adding Christian influences that would not have been present in the original oral traditions.

Key Works

Y Gododdin - An early Welsh poem attributed to the bard Aneirin, commemorating the battle of Catraeth (6th century). It tells the story of warriors from the Kingdom of Gododdin (modern-day Scotland) who fought against Anglo-Saxon forces. Themes include heroism, loyalty, and the tragic consequences of war.

Beowulf - An Old English epic poem about the hero Beowulf from Geatland (modern-day Sweden) who helps King Hrothgar by defeating the monster Grendel and later Grendel's mother. After becoming king, Beowulf faces a dragon, slaying it but receiving mortal wounds. The poem celebrates bravery and heroism.

Renaissance

14th century - 17th century

The Renaissance saw an explosion of artistic and cultural activity emerging from years of plague, famine, and political instability. Beginning in Italy in the 14th century and spreading across Europe, numerous discoveries in science, astrology, and geography brought sweeping changes to society.

A growing interest in humanist philosophy caused intellectuals to revisit the classical works of antiquity. Around 1440, Johannes Gutenberg invented the printing press, allowing literature to be reproduced and distributed on an unprecedented scale, producing up to 3,600 pages per workday compared to the previous hand-copying methods.

Key Works

Don Quixote by Miguel de Cervantes - Widely considered the first example of a modern novel. It follows Alonso Quixano who, after reading too many chivalric romances, becomes a knight-errant named Don Quixote. Accompanied by Sancho Panza, he sets out to revive chivalry, battling windmills he believes to be giants. The novel is a satirical exploration of idealism and reality.

Shakespeare's Plays - William Shakespeare established himself as one of the greatest English language writers with plays like Romeo and Juliet and Hamlet. He wrote approximately 38 plays, which are still widely read and performed today.

Enlightenment and Romanticism

17th century - Mid-19th century

During the Enlightenment, the volume of printed material significantly increased across Europe. Society placed greater importance on reason, individuality, and education. Science and philosophy flourished with great thinkers such as Thomas Hobbes, John Locke, and Jean-Jacques Rousseau producing influential texts.

By the end of the 18th century, European writers began to reject the rationalism of the Enlightenment in favor of sentimentality and imagination. Starting in Germany in the late 1700s and emphasizing individual expression in art and individual rights in politics, Romanticism became Europe's dominant literary movement by the beginning of the 19th century.

Key Enlightenment Works

Hobbes' The Elements of Law, Natural and Politic (1640)

Locke's An Essay Concerning Human Understanding (1689)

Rousseau's The Social Contract (1762)

Key Romantic Works

Les Misérables by Victor Hugo - A sweeping novel set in 19th century France following Jean Valjean, an ex-convict seeking redemption after serving 19 years for stealing bread. The novel explores themes of justice, mercy, love, and revolution against the backdrop of social injustice and French uprisings.

Works by Johann Wolfgang von Goethe and William Blake - Blake's "Songs of Innocence and Experience" and other Romantic works emphasized emotions, nature, and imagination.

Interesting Fact

European Romanticism influenced a similar yet distinct literary movement in the United States called American Romanticism, commonly referred to as the first truly American literary movement, which emphasized the newness and optimism of the Western Frontier.

Modernism

Late 19th century - Early 20th century

Towards the end of the 19th century, realism gave way to modernism, a literary movement that continued the realist tradition of social critique but focused on form and style of writing instead of plot and content. This resulted in new narrative techniques including stream of consciousness narration, non-linear storylines, and multiple viewpoints.

Modernist writers explored the effects of industrialization, war, and new technologies on society. Authors like James Joyce, Franz Kafka, and Virginia Woolf broke away from past literary conventions and experimented with their works' form, style, and structure.

Key Works

Joseph Conrad's Heart of Darkness

T.S. Eliot's The Wasteland

James Joyce's Ulysses

Virginia Woolf's Mrs. Dalloway

Postmodernism & Contemporary

Post-1945

The end of World War II in 1945 generally marks the start of the postmodern period. Postmodern authors use techniques like intertextuality, parody, and metafiction to continue and expand literary experimentation.

  • Intertextuality: A literary convention where an author uses various techniques to refer to and connect the text to another.
  • Parody: An exaggerated imitation of a work or style for comedic effect and to mock or draw attention to specific elements.
  • Metafiction: A technique where the author draws attention to the constructed nature of the text itself.

In European literature, many sub-movements emerged among postmodern authors, such as the Theater of the Absurd, developed in reaction to the senselessness of World War II. This movement, prominent in the 1950s and 60s, presented the belief that life was essentially meaningless.

Key Works

Waiting for Godot by Samuel Beckett (1953) - Often cited as one of the most important works in the Theater of the Absurd movement, capturing the essential themes of existentialism and the meaninglessness of life.
